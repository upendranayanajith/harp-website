Real-World Impact

What Goes Wrong When Gauges Are Misread?

Pressure gauges, flow meters, temperature dials, and analog clocks form the backbone of industrial sensing in petrochemical, water-treatment, and power-generation facilities worldwide.

Despite decades of digital transformation, over 200 million analog instruments remain in active use — the majority in ageing plants that predate digital sensor adoption. Replacing them is neither economically nor logistically feasible. Automated computer vision reading is therefore the most viable path to remote monitoring.

A 2024 incident: A US petrochemical plant suffered a 6-hour halt costing USD 47,000 after a computer vision system misidentified a needle's shadow as a pressure spike. Industry-wide, this happens approximately 18 times per facility per year.

The problem is not that AI cannot detect lines — it is that it cannot tell the difference between a meaningful line and a meaningless shadow, and it cannot verify that its angle reading is physically plausible.

💸

🧠
No physics validation — no system checks whether a predicted reading obeys clock mechanics.

Classical Approaches

Hough Transform Pipelines

Detect circular dials and straight needle lines using the classical Hough transform [2]. Fast, but severely brittle under shadows, perspective distortion, and varying illumination.

Template Matching

Match the detected needle against a library of canonical gauge images. Requires storing hundreds of templates and degrades immediately on unseen designs.

CNN Angle Regression

Zhao et al. [3] applied CNNs to predict gauge angle directly, gaining 12–18 percentage points over Hough. But regresses a scalar over [0°, 360°) — introducing the circular discontinuity bias.

The Gap

What Is Missing in Current Solutions?

Three specific technical gaps — not covered by any prior work — are directly addressed by HARP.

🎯
Gap 1

No Semantic Shadow Rejection

Every prior automated meter reading system operates purely on pixel-level features. None exploit the semantic meaning of what a pointer represents to distinguish it from its shadow. The difference is not visual — it is conceptual.

FPR in prior work: 12–34%

📐
Gap 2

No Geometrically Correct Uncertainty

Existing uncertainty estimates use Euclidean standard deviation on circular data — which is geometrically incorrect. A prediction of 1° and 359° appear maximally uncertain but are only 2° apart. Mardia–Jupp circular statistics solve this; no prior gauge-reading work applies them.

⚙️
Gap 3

No Physics-Guided Validation

No existing clock- or gauge-reading system validates its predicted reading against physical constraints. A clock cannot show 3:75. The hour hand must be shorter. These constraints can be checked exhaustively in under 15 ms — yet no prior work does this.

Research Objectives

What We Set Out to Achieve

Design a zero-shot shadow discrimination method using vision-language model embedding coherence that requires no task-specific training data and generalises across all gauge types and illumination conditions. Target: FPR below 10%.

Replace scalar angle regression with a directional sin/cos parameterisation that eliminates the 0°/360° wraparound discontinuity. Add calibrated epistemic uncertainty using geometrically correct Mardia–Jupp circular statistics. Target: median CAE below 5°.

Build a physics-guided reasoning engine that exhaustively evaluates all 720 valid clock readings and selects the one most consistent with kinematic constraints. Assign structured confidence labels. Target: exact-minute accuracy above 90%.

Design a dual-model detection system that distinguishes clock faces from industrial gauges, and filters low-quality inputs using a composite image-quality index before they reach downstream modules.

Integrate all four components into a single end-to-end pipeline that operates within a 300 ms real-time budget on a mid-range GPU, and validate in real industrial environments across three distinct facility types over at least six weeks.

Approach

How HARP Solves the Problem — Step by Step

STEP 1 — C1

Detect & Gate

Two independent YOLOv8 models process each image in parallel — one trained for clock faces, one for industrial gauges. The winning model's bounding box is selected. A composite quality score then decides whether the image is sharp, bright, and clear enough to proceed. Poor images are flagged before wasting compute on later stages.

mAP@0.5
55%
Error Reduction from Gating
STEP 2 — C2 (SSF)

Find the Real Needle

YOLO-Pose detects candidate keypoints (both real and shadow). For each candidate, SSF renders a hypothesis: "what would this image look like if this candidate were the real needle?" It then asks CLIP — a model trained on 400 million images — whether that hypothesis looks like a valid gauge. Shadows fail this test; real needles pass it.

93%

STEP 3 — C3

Estimate the Angle Correctly

Instead of predicting "352°" (which has a hard numerical discontinuity with "4°"), C3 predicts the sine and cosine of the angle. These are continuous everywhere on the circle. Twenty random forward passes through the neural network (MC-Dropout) give a probability distribution. The circular standard deviation tells downstream modules how confident the estimate is.

STEP 4 — C4

Pick the Right Time Using Physics

A clock can only show one of 720 valid times. C4 computes the theoretical hand positions for every single one and finds which time best matches what C3 estimated. It also checks: is the shorter hand the hour hand? Do the hands make sense together? The output is a verified reading with a confidence label (CERTAIN / CONFIDENT / UNCERTAIN / UNRELIABLE).

Solution & Product

The HARP System — Technical Deep Dive

From raw camera image to a confidence-scored, physics-validated instrument reading in under 300 milliseconds.

Component C1

Dual-Model Detection & Quality Gating

The pipeline begins with two independent YOLOv8 detectors — one specialised for clock faces (trained on 4,200 images across 47 clock models) and one for industrial gauges (2,800 images). Both run in parallel; the one with higher confidence wins.

Critically, a high detection confidence does not mean the image is usable. A blurry gauge at 95% confidence still cannot be read accurately. C1 computes a composite quality score Q from blur (Laplacian variance), brightness, and contrast. Images below Q = 0.45 are flagged before wasting compute downstream.

Component C2 — Novel Contribution

Semantic Shadow Filtering (SSF)

This is HARP's most novel contribution. SSF is the first zero-shot shadow discrimination method for gauge skeleton extraction. It requires no shadow-labelled training data and generalises across all gauge types and lighting environments.

The key insight is analysis-by-synthesis: for each detected keypoint candidate, the system renders what the image would look like if that candidate were the real needle. It then uses CLIP (pretrained on 400 million images) to score whether that rendered hypothesis is semantically coherent with a valid gauge image. Shadows fail this test; real needles pass.

A three-way decision separates clear accepts (≥ 0.73), clear rejects (< 0.43), and borderline cases routed to human review. Operators resolved 89% of flagged cases in under 2 seconds using the rendered hypothesis as visual context.

Component C3

Directional Regression with Epistemic Uncertainty

Standard AI models predict angles as plain numbers like "352°". The problem: 352° and 4° are only 12° apart in reality, but numerically they differ by 348°. This inflates the training loss near midnight/12-o'clock and biases every nearby prediction.

C3 solves this by predicting the sine and cosine of the angle instead — a representation that wraps naturally around the circle. Monte Carlo Dropout runs 20 random forward passes to produce a probability distribution. The spread of those passes (measured using Mardia–Jupp circular statistics) gives a reliable confidence score that propagates into C4.

Component C4

Physics-Guided Symbolic Reasoning

C4 is what makes HARP more than just a detector. It knows that a clock can only display one of exactly 720 valid times, and it knows the precise mathematical relationship between the angle of each hand and the time it encodes.

For every one of those 720 candidate times, C4 calculates where the hands should be, and computes how far that is from what C3 estimated. The best-matching candidate wins. This resolves ambiguous cases — like distinguishing 3:15 from 3:20 — that visual estimation alone cannot reliably separate.

Every output comes with a confidence label and a diagnostic explanation string, satisfying industrial safety documentation requirements (IEC 61511).
